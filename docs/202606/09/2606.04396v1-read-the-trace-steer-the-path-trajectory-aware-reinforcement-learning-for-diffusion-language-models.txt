Abstract 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) gen-erate responses by iteratively unmasking and revising many positions in parallel. This pro-cess leaves a rich denoising trace depicting which tokens become confident, which remain unstable, and when commitments form. Ex-isting dLLM reinforcement learning methods use this signal only weakly. Flat rollouts are cheap, but assign a single outcome reward to the whole trajectory. Tree rollouts provide finer, verifiable training signals by branching par-tial trajectories and propagating leaf rewards upward, but is compute intensive. We ask whether the denoising trace itself can provide tree-like supervision without tree-level com-pute. We introduce CAPR ( Cached-Amortized 

Path Refinement), a dLLM-RL algorithm that summarizes the denoising trace into a compact path state, uses cached trajectory states to gen-erate cheap sibling continuations, and trains a block-level value head for local block-wise supervision. Under a block-wise unmasking schedule, CAPR records path-state and block-progress features, then redistributes the final outcome reward across blocks according to the tokens revealed in each block. This trains the value head to convert one sparse reward into block-level PPO weights. CAPR therefore re-covers much of the granularity of tree search while avoiding full tree expansion, reducing rollout-generation cost to roughly 0.75 × of flat rollouts and 0.6 × of tree rollouts (at stan-dard setting). Across 4×4 Sudoku, Countdown, GSM8K, and Math500, on dense and mixture-of-experts LLaDA backbones, CAPR sets a new state of the art for RL-tuned dLLMs at 256- and 512-token budgets. On Sudoku, it matches the strongest tree-structured baseline at less than one third of the per-step compute. 

1 Introduction 

Diffusion large language models (dLLMs) (Nie et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025b) generate text by de-noising a fully masked sequence over many parallel steps. At every step, the model exposes a predic-tive distribution for every masked position. This is a rich trace of how the answer forms, but current reinforcement learning (RL) based post-training for dLLMs (Zhao et al., 2025a; Zhu et al., 2025a; Tang et al., 2026; Xie et al., 2026; Pan et al., 2026) uses little of it. An end-of-generation reward must still be assigned across many denoising steps and token blocks, so the learner has no direct signal for which step fixed an important token and which step merely changed an irrelevant one. Existing RL methods for dLLMs sit at two ex-tremes. Flat-rollout methods assign the same out-come reward to all steps. They are cheap, but they cannot tell which denoising decisions mat-tered. Tree-rollout methods (Pan et al., 2026) fork alternative continuations and compare their final rewards. They obtain finer advantages, but their cost grows quickly with branching factor and depth. Figure 1 (right) shows this cost-granularity trade-off. The denoising trace offers a third route. At each step, a position either becomes more stable and confident, or it keeps changing. Figure 1 (left) illustrates this on a simple prompt (“ 7+5 :”). By step three the tens digit has settled at 1, while the units digit is still oscillating. The trace therefore tells us which positions are becoming committed, without running a tree search. The key question is how to convert this trace into a learning signal. We answer this question with CAPR (Cached-

Amortized Path Refinement), an RL algorithm for block-based dLLMs. CAPR consists of three broad mechanisms. a) Cache & Steer : CAPR records the early denoising steps, builds a compact path state from confidence and stability at each posi-tion, and reuses this cached prefix for producing sibling rollouts or branches. The path state is used immediately at every denoising step to steer the next-step logits, reinforcing stable commitments and damping oscillating ones. b) Branch & Prune :At a fixed mid-trajectory step, CAPR branches a

Contributions. 

• A denoising trace-based view of RL for masked dLLMs: confidence and stability along the de-noising path can be turned into per-block advan-tages, without full tree expansion. • CAPR, a block-based RL algorithm that caches early denoising outputs, amortizes sibling roll-outs from the cached prefix, steers each next de-noising step with a path state, and uses a block critic to turn the final reward into one advantage per block for PPO. • State-of-the-art results for RL-tuned dLLMs at both 256-token and 512-token decoding bud-gets across four reasoning benchmarks and two LLaDA backbones, with less than a third of the per-step compute of the strongest tree-structured baseline on Sudoku. 

2 Related Work 

Diffusion language models. Diffusion language models generate text by iteratively denoising masked tokens rather than predicting strictly left to right (Nie et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025b; Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2025a; Ye et al., 2025b,a; Labs et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). This decoding process exposes a distribution over every masked position at every step, which makes dLLMs a natural setting for trace-based training sig-nals. Recent open models and paradigms, including LLaDA-1.5 (Zhu et al., 2025a), LLaDA-MoE (Zhu et al., 2025b), Dream-7B (Ye et al., 2025a), and SDAR (Cheng et al., 2025), have made dLLMs strong reasoning backbones. We use the dense and MoE LLaDA variants in our experiments. 

RL post-training for dLLMs. Most RL methods for dLLMs differ in how they assign an outcome reward to the denoising process. Flat-rollout meth-ods, including Diffu-GRPO (Zhao et al., 2025a), VRPO (Zhu et al., 2025a), wd1 (Tang et al., 2026), and d2-stepMerge (Wang et al., 2026), keep sam-pling cheap by applying one trajectory-level reward to all token updates. This makes the update sim-ple, but it does not identify which denoising steps or blocks mattered. Step-aware methods such as SAPO (Xie et al., 2026) and TraceRL (Wang et al., 2025) attach more local signals inside a rollout, but they do not use the denoising trace to learn a block-level value function. Tree-based methods, especially d-TreeRPO (Pan et al., 2026), compare branched continuations and produce segment-level advantages. They are effective, but the cost grows rapidly with branching factor and depth. CAPR targets the gap between these regimes. It keeps the rollout structure close to flat sampling, but uses the trace to produce one advantage per block. Concurrent work explores related ways to stabi-lize or enrich dLLM-RL, including consistency-trajectory regularization (Yang et al., 2025a), lateral-thought reinforcement (Huang et al., 2025b), multimodal RL for dLLMs (Yang et al., 2025b), entropy and anchoring objectives (Yang et al., 2026; Cui et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026), boundary-aware updates (Lin et al., 2025), MDPO (He et al., 2025), principled denoising objectives (Ou et al., 2025), and inpainting-style RL (Zhao et al., 2025b). CAPR is complementary to these direc-tions. Its main contribution is to convert the denois-ing trace into reusable path states and per-block PPO weights. 

Connections to policy optimization and guided decoding. CAPR’s block critic is a lightweight actor–critic component (Sutton, 1988; Schulman et al., 2016, 2017) adapted to sparse, outcome-only rewards. It reads features that are specific to masked decoding: path-state statistics, block progress, prompt-level reward statistics, and the tokens revealed so far. A slowly-updated target copy (Lillicrap et al., 2016) and a small replay buffer keep this online value head stable. The steering shift is related to guided decoding, but it does not use an external classifier or reward model. It uses the model’s own trace. Stable, confi-dent positions receive a small positive bias on the next denoising step, while oscillating positions are damped. The form follows the additive logit con-trol induced by linearly-solvable MDPs (Todorov, 2006). Finally, CAPR uses a one-sided χ2 trust region to suppress rare token-level log-ratio spikes without penalizing all policy movement equally. This is related in spirit to asymmetric clipping (Yu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2026) and policy-gradient stabilization (Cui et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Tan et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025), but it is ap-plied to token-level dLLM updates with an explicit upper-tail trust-region interpretation. 

3 Method 

3.1 Preliminaries 

A masked dLLM πθ generates a completion y for prompt q over N denoising steps, moving from 

zN = [ q; [MASK] L] to z0 = [ q; y]. Block decod-ing partitions the L completion tokens into blocks. Flat GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) optimizes a clipped policy-gradient objective with a rollout-level out-come advantage shared by all tokens in the comple-tion. Tree methods (Pan et al., 2026) obtain more local advantages by comparing branched contin-uations. CAPR keeps the sampling pattern close to flat GRPO, but replaces the single rollout-level advantage with one advantage per block. We write 

rij (θ) = πθ(yij )/π θold (yij ) for the token-level im-portance ratio used by the PPO update in CAPR. The CAPR methodology is illustrated in Fig. 2. During the early denoising steps it records how each masked position behaves across steps. Sta-ble, confident positions are treated as emerging commitments, oscillating positions are treated as unresolved. This record is compressed into a path state. The path state is then used in three ways: it steers the transition zt to zt−1, later branches reuse a cached prefix, and it gives the block critic the information needed to turn one final reward into one advantage per block.

3.3 BRANCH & P RUNE 

To explore alternative completions (see App. D), and to assign granular rewards for every block in every branch, at low compute cost, we perform branching at the midpoint of the denoising trajec-tory in CAPR. To reduce compute costs for each branch, CAPR caches a prefix so that these sibling continuations (i.e. branches) can start without any extra forward pass. The prefix is a combination of logits distribution at the branch step, along with the predicted tokens, and path state h(t).At a fixed fraction fb (we set it to 0.5) of the denoising budget N , it forks Gb branches from the shared prefix. The fork reuses the stored branch-step prefix and changes only the predictions for the Gb branches by sampling a different Gv for every vocab token v in each branch. This pruning is deliberately local and cheap. It does not require evaluating a reward model at interme-diate steps, it uses the same trace signal already maintained by “Cache & Steer”. Tree method branches many times and compares final rewards of many continuations, but this ex-pands compute quickly. CAPR instead creates a small local comparison set by forking sibling con-tinuations from a cached mid-trajectory state. Since the siblings share the same prefix and differ only in the suffix, their outcomes and path-state quality give useful evidence about which later denoising decisions are promising. Pruning them prevents the small branch set from growing into a full tree. 

3.4 BLOCK CRITIC 

The block critic answers a simple question: after seeing only the final reward, which blocks were most useful? At each block boundary, CAPR stores a compact state containing the path state, block progress (number of unmasked tokens within the block), prompt-level reward statistics, and tokens revealed so far. Using this compact state, the value head is trained as discussed in App. A.2. After the rollout finishes, CAPR redistributes the final reward R over blocks according to how many tokens each block revealed, with the final block also receiving the terminal outcome signal. Inference. At deployment, CAPR uses only the trained LoRA policy with the standard single-path dLLM denoising loop. Cache & Steer, branching, pruning, the block critic, and self-distillation are training-only mechanisms. Their signal has already been distilled into the policy weights at train time. Inference therefore uses the same number of model forwards as the base dLLM, with no value head or training-time path state.

5 Conclusion 

Masked diffusion language models expose a denois-ing trace that standard RL post-training largely ig-nores. CAPR turns this trace into a training signal. It records confidence, entropy, and token stability in a compact path state, uses that state to steer re-verse denoising steps, reuses cached prefixes to cre-ate cheap sibling continuations, and trains a Block Critic to convert one final outcome reward into per-block PPO weights. In this way, CAPR preserves the locality of tree-style comparisons without ex-panding a full search tree. Across dense and mixture-of-experts LLaDA backbones, CAPR improves reasoning under the same decoding setting as the baselines. At the matched 256-token budget, it is best or tied-best on all reported tasks, with the largest gains on Sudoku and Countdown, where flat rollout-level signals are weakest. The 512-token results show that the same pattern holds at longer generation lengths. On Sudoku, CAPR matches the strongest tree-structured baseline while using less than one third of its per-step wall-clock time. The ablations show that the gains do not come from branching alone. Branching and pruning add useful diversity, the Block Critic provides the largest intermediate improvement by localizing the reward signal, self-distillation keeps solved groups useful after group-relative advantages vanish, and the chi-square regu-larizer limits unstable ratio spikes. CAPR therefore offers a practical middle ground for dLLM-RL, with finer reward assignment than flat rollouts at substantially lower cost than full tree expansion. 

Limitations 

CAPR is evaluated on automatically verifiable rea-soning tasks. This is a controlled setting for study-ing reward assignment, but it does not establish performance for open-ended instruction following, factuality, dialogue safety, or preference-based ob-jectives where rewards are noisier and less local. The experiments also use zero-shot pass@1 decod-ing, pass@ k evaluation, robustness under different sampling settings, and out-of-domain prompts re-main important tests of generalization. The block critic is learned online from the same rollouts used to update the policy. This keeps CAPR lightweight, but the value estimates may be less reliable for very long reasoning chains, de-layed subgoals, or domains where many partial tra-jectories can lead to the same final reward. CAPR is also tested only on LLaDA-family masked diffu-sion language models. Other dLLM architectures may expose different trace statistics, and branch fraction, block size, self-distillation weight, and value-head calibration may need retuning. Finally, the compute measurements reflect our software stack and 8×A100-80GB hardware. CAPR reduces denoising continuations relative to tree expansion, but it still requires RL fine-tuning of large language models. Memory layout, kernel efficiency, and distributed-training overhead can change wall-clock cost on other systems. 

Ethical Considerations 

CAPR is a training algorithm for improving rea-soning in masked diffusion language models. The experiments use public reasoning benchmarks with automatically checkable rewards, and we do not introduce new user data or sensitive annotations. However, stronger reasoning models can be mis-used in the same ways as other capable language models, including producing misleading explana-tions, assisting harmful workflows, or making in-correct answers appear more persuasive. CAPR does not add a safety-alignment layer, so user-facing deployments should retain the safeguards, monitoring, refusal behaviour, and domain-specific evaluations required for the underlying model. RL fine-tuning large models also has an envi-ronmental cost. CAPR is designed to reduce train-ing cost relative to tree-structured rollouts, and we report hardware and wall-clock measurements to make that cost visible. Future work should continue to report compute transparently and study whether trajectory-aware signals can be reused across tasks, seeds, or training runs. We downloaded models from huggingface. A.4 Chi-Square Regulariser 

Sparse-reward updates can fail because of a few tokens, not because the whole completion moves too far. In a solved rollout, PPO may strongly in-crease the probability of a small number of already-likely tokens. These upper-tail log-ratio spikes can dominate the gradient, especially when most other rollouts have zero or identical reward. Standard PPO clipping and KL regularisation (Kakade and Langford, 2002; Schulman et al., 2017) control av-erage policy movement, but they do not specifically target this token-level upper tail. D.3 Why this is Stable 

Two safeguards keep the learned signal from domi-nating when it is unreliable. First, the target value head V¯ϕ is a slow Polyak average of the online head, which smooths noisy block targets. Second, CAPR gates the TD blend using the recent value loss: when the value head is poorly calibrated, βeff TD 

becomes small and the update falls back toward the flat GRPO advantage. The self-distillation loss and chi-square regu-lariser are local to the policy update and are de-fined in Appendix A.3 and Appendix A.4. Self-distillation copies solved rollouts back into the LoRA policy, which is why the value head and search machinery can be removed at inference. 

D.4 Implication of the Bound 

CAPR trades tree depth for a learned block predic-tor. When the value head explains a nonzero part of the final-reward variance, the resulting block advantage has lower noise than a flat rollout advan-tage at nearly the same generation cost. When the value head is weak, the calibration gate reduces its influence, so CAPR reverts toward the flat baseline rather than relying on an inaccurate local signal. E Detailed Descriptions of dLLM RL Baselines 

This appendix summarises the baselines in Table 1. For each method, we describe its training signal, the advantage assigned to token updates, and the main approximation used for masked diffusion like-lihoods. The goal is to make the comparison with CAPR interpretable rather than to reproduce the original derivations. 

Diffu-GRPO (Zhao et al., 2025a). A direct port of GRPO to masked dLLMs. The reward is the ver-ifier outcome on the final completion oi, and the ad-vantage Ai,k = ri − mean {rj }Gj=1 is broadcast to every token of oi. The only dLLM-specific piece is the log-probability estimator: rather than marginal-ising over denoising orders, Diffu-GRPO evalu-ates log πθ in a single forward pass on a randomly masked prompt and a fully masked completion, and re-samples the prompt mask each inner update for regularisation. This makes training cheap but gives every token the same rollout-level signal. CAPR instead uses the per-block advantages of §3.4. 

VRPO (Zhu et al., 2025a). A DPO-style prefer-ence optimiser using offline pairs (q, o w, o l) and no explicit reward model. Since log πθ(o | q)

is intractable, VRPO substitutes an ELBO bound 

Bπ(o | q) ≤ log π(o | q) and forms the DPO score from differences of Bπθ − Bπref on ow and ol.Two ideas reduce the variance of this estimator: an optimal allocation of the sampling budget across diffusion timesteps (more nt, fewer nzt ) and anti-thetic sampling that shares noise between the live and reference models. The remaining gap is struc-tural: the score is built on a lower bound, not the true log-likelihood. VRPO is the recipe behind the public LLaDA-1.5 release on LLaDA-8B-Instruct. 

wd1 (Tang et al., 2026). A ratio-free alterna-tive to GRPO. After scoring a group with the verifier, wd1 maps the group-relative advantages
